Time Series - Predciting Avocado Prices

by Hansel Poe

Time is like a river made up of the events which happen, and a violent stream; for as soon as a thing has been seen, it is carried away, and another comes in its place, and this will be carried away too.
- Marcus Aurelius, Meditations

The quote above is from the stoic philosopher, Marcus Aurelius, in his book, Meditations, written almost 2000 years ago. And although Marcus probably wasn't thinking of machine learning when he wrote this, he offers some insight into the changing nature of the world. Indeed, things are in constant flux, and events occur one after another: Seasons change, the stock market fluctuates, the weather changes, and so on.

The question we're interested in is:

Given information about a series of events which has happened, how can we use ML to predict about things that have not happened?

Given a set of data points indexed by time, a Time Series, we can train a ML model to learn patterns and make predictions about the future. This means that we can predict things such as: weather forecasts, stock market projections, powerplant grid energy demands, and other useful information.

In this blog, we'll dive into a practical example of using machine learning to predict avocado prices.

We will use a linear model called Ridge. In high school, we learnt the linear equation, $$y= mx + c$$ Ridge works similarly, where we have $y$ as the thing we are trying to predict (AveragePriceNextWeek), but instead of having just one $m$ and $x$ we have one these for each column (except AveragePriceNextWeek). The idea here is that we make a prediction, we sum up the product of each value in the column with their coefficient $m$ and the intercept $c$ to get our prediction $y$, or more explicitly, we compute :

$$y = m_1x_1 + m_2x_2 + \dots + m_nx_n$$

where $n$ is our number of columns, excluding AveragePriceNextWeek.

When we train the model on our train set, we learn our $m$'s for each column and the intercept $c$.

But one last thing before we train our model, we will add a new column Season that will describe the season of each row based on the month of Date:

  • Winter - June, July, August
  • Autumn - March, April, May
  • Summer - December, January, February
  • Fall - September, October, November

We will also apply some preprocessing to make our model work better, namely, scaling our numeric features and encoding our categorical columns. The details of the preprocessing is not important, but just note that we might see new columns in our final result, due to our preprocessing.

Conclusion

And there it goes! we just trained our own model on a time series and use it to predict on future events. We also gain some useful insights from the coefficients about which seasons the average price of avocados might be higher. There are many ways to encode information in our time series than just seasons. We may for example, add a new column giving us the average price of avocados the week before. This may lead to different scores and maybe even different coefficients. We can also use a different model than Ridge.

Notes

  • Note that the model we used may not necessarily perform much better than a baseline, but it's still pretty useful in giving us insights about how seasons impact avocado prices.
  • When splitting the time series, we sort the dataset and choose the last 6 months as our test set, this is important as we are treating our test set as the 'future' we are trying to predict, we must not have data in our training set that is later than data in our test set. This would cause some sort of data leakage.
  • By using a linear model, we assume that each season will have a linear relationship with the model, but realistically that may not be the case. Perhaps it's possible that the fall season for different years may have different coefficients. We can engineer interaction columns between year and season which may give us an even better model and more descriptive columns, such as "Fall 2024"
